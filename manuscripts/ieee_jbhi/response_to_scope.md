# Response to IEEE/JBHI Scope

This manuscript fits IEEE Journal of Biomedical and Health Informatics as a biomedical AI validation study for respiratory-audio screening under distribution shift. The contribution is not a new clinical diagnostic device. It is an integrated validation framework for assessing whether COVID-19 respiratory-audio models remain reliable under participant separation, temporal drift, external dataset shift, and metadata shortcut risk.

The health-informatics relevance is threefold. First, the study examines model behavior across participant-disjoint, time-aware, strict early-to-late temporal, and external-transfer validation protocols. Second, it quantifies metadata confounding through metadata-only baselines, including AUROC 0.964 for full safe metadata, AUROC 0.932 for symptoms-only metadata, and AUROC 0.914 for demographic/protocol-only metadata. Third, it interprets respiratory-audio COVID-19 modeling as a reliability and reporting problem rather than as a single internal benchmark.

The manuscript is intentionally conservative about clinical implications. It reports internal Coswara multimodal AUROC 0.897, time-stratified AUROC 0.849, strict temporal AUROC 0.698, and a model-matched cough-only drop from Coswara internal AUROC 0.849-0.868 to COUGHVID external-transfer AUROC 0.523-0.543, showing that internal discrimination does not establish deployment readiness. COUGHVID is explicitly scoped as cough-only transfer and not as multimodal validation. The revised manuscript also includes a claim-control table so readers can see which biomedical claim is supported by each validation protocol. This scope aligns with JBHI interests in trustworthy biomedical machine learning, validation under distribution shift, and clinically cautious health-informatics evaluation.
